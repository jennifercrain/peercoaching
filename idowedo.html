Building Skills Through Stages

Effective learning happens in steps. Instead of jumping straight to independence, students benefit from watching, collaborating, and then practicing on their own.

👤

I Do

You demonstrate the strategy while narrating your decision-making. This is where students see your thinking process in action.

👥

We Do

Work through the strategy together, with you providing guidance as the student participates. They're starting to take ownership.

🎯

You Do

The student tackles the strategy solo. They've internalized the approach and are ready to handle it on their own.

💡

The Real Power of "I Do"

It's not about teaching the subject—it's about revealing your thinking behind the strategy. Students need to see how you decide what matters, what to skip, and how you organize information. They're learning your mental process, not just the outcome.

Making Thinking Visible: Think Alouds

🧠

What's a Think Aloud?

Imagine a student watching you work on a problem, but instead of just seeing the final answer, they get to hear every thought in your head. That's a think aloud. You're narrating your reasoning out loud—the decisions you make, what grabs your attention, what confuses you, and why you skip certain things.

When you think aloud, you share:

👁️ What stands out to you
⭐ What seems worth paying attention to
❓ What feels confusing or unclear
✂️ Why you keep certain things and skip others
📂 How you structure and categorize ideas
✅ How you test yourself to make sure you get it

Why This Changes Everything

When students only see your finished notes or final answer, they have no idea how you got there. A think aloud bridges that gap. It shows them your approach and strategy, not just the result. They can then copy your process, not just your output.

Think alouds help you model:

→ Tackling dense or unfamiliar material without freezing up
→ Deciding what's worth writing down versus what's fluff
→ Putting complex ideas into plain language you understand
→ Breaking big concepts into smaller, manageable chunks
→ Turning material into study formats like charts, cards, or concept maps

Putting It Into Practice

Ready to run your own think aloud session? Here's what you need to do to set one up effectively.

1

Start with Real Material

Pick something your student actually needs to learn—a passage, a problem, a slide. Whatever they're actually struggling with.

2

Have Your Strategy Tool Ready

Will they be taking notes? Making a chart? Creating flashcards? Have the template or blank page right there so they can see you fill it in as you think.

3

Keep the Scope Tight

Don't try to model the entire chapter. A paragraph or small section is perfect. You want to showcase the technique, not overwhelm them with content.

4

Work Out Loud (Really Out Loud!)

This is the core of it. As you work, narrate everything—what you're noticing, what you're skipping and why, how you're deciding what matters. Don't filter it.

5

Show Your Translation Process

Explain why you're rewriting something, why you're grouping ideas that way, why you're leaving certain details out. Explain the reasoning behind your choices.

⏱️

A good think aloud doesn't need to be long. A few minutes showing your approach is way more powerful than twenty minutes of explanation.
