Peer Coaching Strategy

I Do & Think Alouds

Modeling How to Use a Study Strategy

In Peer Coaching, we model effective learning processes so students can see what it looks like to approach material strategically and independently.

One of the most effective ways to do this is through the "I Do" stage—the first part of a gradual release of responsibility that helps students internalize not just what to do, but how to think while they're doing it.

The I Do, We Do, You Do Framework

When introducing a study strategy, peer coaches use a gradual release of responsibility:

I Do: The peer coach models the strategy in real time.
You show what's going on in a learner's head when they use a strategy, how they decide what matters, and how they organize their thinking.
We Do: The peer coach and student try the strategy together.
You work side-by-side with guided support as the student begins applying what they observed.
You Do: The student applies the strategy independently.
The student practices the strategy on their own, building confidence and mastery.

The goal of the "I Do" stage isn't to teach the course material. It's to show the process instead of just the final result.

What Is a Think Aloud?

A think aloud is the practice of verbalizing your internal thinking while you work through a problem. Instead of silently reading, summarizing, or organizing, you speak the process out loud:

  • • What you notice
  • • What feels important
  • • What seems confusing
  • • How you decide what to keep or condense
  • • How you organize information
  • • How you check your own understanding

Think alouds externalize internal thinking. They allow students to observe how someone approaches material, not just what the final product looks like.

How to Set Up an "I Do" with a Think Aloud

  1. 1.
    Open the course material.

    This might be a short reading, a slide, a practice problem, a rubric, or a small section of a textbook.

  2. 2.
    Have the strategy template available.

    For example: a task table, Cornell notes, a flashcard template, a concept map, or a study matrix.

  3. 3.
    Select a small, relevant portion.

    Choose material directly connected to the student's challenge. Keep it brief so the focus stays on the process, not the volume of content.

  4. 4.
    Model using the strategy while thinking out loud.

    Narrate your thinking: what you notice, what you decide is important, how you interpret information, and how you organize it.

  5. 5.
    Translate the material into the strategy format.

    Reword, condense, chunk, and reorganize while explaining why you're making each move.

Think alouds generally account for only a few minutes of the coaching session.

What a Think Aloud Might Sound Like

Below are example stems that illustrate how a peer coach might verbalize their internal process while modeling a strategy:

"As I look at this section, the first thing I notice is how many key terms are introduced close together."
"This paragraph is pretty dense, so I'm going to slow down and look for the main idea in each sentence."
"I'm noticing that this definition is long, which tells me I'll probably need to condense it for my notes."
"I'm asking myself what I would actually need to remember from this later."
"This example helps, but the core idea seems to be this sentence here."
"I'm separating the big idea from the supporting details."
"I wouldn't copy this word-for-word. I'm going to put it in my own words so it's easier to recall."
"I'm shortening this into a one-line summary that still keeps the meaning."
"I'm breaking this long explanation into two smaller pieces so it's easier to study."
"Since I'm using flashcards, I'm putting the key term on one side and a short, clear cue on the other."
"In a table, I'm deciding which column this belongs in based on what kind of information it is."
"In an outline, this feels like a main point, and these would be supporting details underneath."
"As I write this, I'm checking whether this would make sense to me if I came back to it later."
"I'm noticing I'm still a little unsure about this part, so I'd probably flag it to review or ask about."
"I'm asking myself if this summary really captures the idea, or if I need to refine it."

Why Think Alouds Matter in Peer Coaching

Think alouds:

  • ✓ Make invisible thinking visible for students
  • ✓ Model how to engage with material strategically
  • ✓ Show how to move from raw information to usable study tools
  • ✓ Help students see how to monitor their own understanding
  • ✓ Reinforce that effective studying involves active decision-making, not passive reading

During the "I Do" stage, the peer coach is not demonstrating subject-matter expertise. The coach is modeling the process of learning: noticing, selecting, organizing, rewording, and checking understanding.

This prepares students for the next stages:

We Do: They begin applying the same processes with support.
You Do: They apply the strategy independently, using the thinking moves they observed and practiced.

Try It Out: Interactive Examples

Below are scenarios where you can see what a coach's think aloud sounds like in real situations. Click "Show Think Aloud" to reveal how a peer coach might verbalize their thinking process.

Scenario 1: Reading a Dense Textbook Section

Material: A 2-paragraph explanation of photosynthesis from a biology textbook

"Okay, I'm looking at these two paragraphs about photosynthesis. The first thing I notice is there are a lot of scientific terms introduced quickly—chlorophyll, glucose, ATP. That tells me I need to go slowly and not try to memorize everything at once. Let me focus on the main process first: sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide go in, and glucose comes out. That's the big picture. Now I'm going to read the first sentence again to see what the key steps are. I'm noticing that this sentence talks about the light-dependent reactions, and then the next sentence talks about the light-independent reactions. So there are clearly two main stages. I'm going to write those down as my main points, and I'll add details under each one. This way, when I study later, I have the structure already set up."

Scenario 2: Working Through a Math Problem

Material: A step-by-step solution to a quadratic equation

"Looking at this problem, I see a quadratic equation. Before I dive into solving it, I'm asking myself: do I need to understand every step or just the process? I think the process is what matters here. So I'm looking at step one—they factored this expression. I'm going to write down 'factor if possible' as my first checkpoint. In step two, they set each factor equal to zero. I'm noticing that's the rule for the zero product property. That's not something I should memorize word-for-word; I need to understand why we do that. So I'm writing down: 'if ab=0, then a=0 or b=0.' That's the core idea. Then they solve each equation. These are simple one-step equations, so I don't need to spend time there. My key takeaway from this problem is the overall structure, not every detail."

Scenario 3: Creating a Study Tool from a Lecture Slide

Material: A slide covering types of government systems with bullet points and definitions

"This slide lists five types of government. I could copy these definitions word-for-word, but that won't help me when I'm taking the test. Instead, I'm going to ask myself: what's the one key difference between each type? For democracy, the key is that power is held by the people. For monarchy, it's that one person inherits power. For dictatorship, it's that one person takes power by force. See how I'm condensing that? I'm not copying the full definitions; I'm pulling out the distinguishing feature. Now I'm going to make a comparison chart so I can see all five side-by-side. I'll put the government type in one column and the key feature in another. This way, I can quickly compare them and study from the chart instead of from long paragraphs."

Learning Check: Does This Think Aloud Focus on Strategy?

Read each scenario below. Decide whether the speaker is focusing on the learning strategy (process, thinking moves, decision-making) or just presenting content.

Question 1

"The mitochondria is the powerhouse of the cell. It has an outer membrane and an inner membrane. The cristae are folds in the inner membrane that increase surface area for energy production."

Question 2

"I'm going to break this history chapter into smaller chunks because I notice there are three distinct time periods. For each one, I'm asking: What was happening? Why did it happen? What came after? That structure helps me see how events connect. I'm going to make a timeline with these three periods as my main divisions, and then I'll add details under each one."

Question 3

"Here's a list of five government types. I'm reading each definition and I'm wondering: what's the one key feature that makes each different from the others? Instead of memorizing whole definitions, I'm pulling out just that distinguishing feature. So for democracy it's 'power held by people,' for monarchy it's 'inherited power.' I'm going to make a quick comparison chart that shows just these features so I can see them all at once."
