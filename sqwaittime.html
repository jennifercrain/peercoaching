Week Two Training

Staged Questioning & Wait Time

Two powerful skills that keep thinking with the student

What Is Wait Time?

Wait time is the intentional pause you give after asking a question. Instead of rushing to rephrase, explain, or rescue, you allow silence so the student can think.

For the Coach — What Silence Triggers

  • → "They didn't understand me."
  • → "I should say that another way."
  • → "They're stuck — I need to help."
  • → "This is awkward."

For the Student — What Silence Enables

  • ✓ Processing the question
  • ✓ Searching their memory
  • ✓ Organizing their thoughts
  • ✓ Building the confidence to answer
  • ✓ Actively trying to reason it out

What feels like "dead air" to you is often active mental work for them.

What Is Staged Questioning?

Staged questioning means you support the student's thinking in layers instead of jumping straight to the answer. The student remains the one doing the work at every stage.

1

Stage 1 – Ask and Wait (15–20 seconds)

Ask the question clearly and give real thinking time. This is where most thinking happens, and most coaches jump in too quickly.

2

Stage 2 – Rephrase or Invert and Wait (10 seconds)

If there is no response, restate the question in a different way or ask the opposite angle. You're helping them access the thinking from a new entry point, not doing it for them.

3

Stage 3 – Narrow or Hint and Wait (5 seconds)

Only then do you gently narrow the focus or offer a small cue — still without giving the answer. You're narrowing the search space, not solving it.

Practice: Try the Stages with Real Questions

—

Stage 1 – Ask & Wait (15–20s)

What do you think will happen to the result if you change that variable?

Stage 2 – Rephrase & Wait (10s)

If that variable got smaller, how would it affect your answer?

Stage 3 – Narrow & Wait (5s)

Does a bigger number make it go up or down?

The peer coach is guiding without giving the answer away.

Real Examples: Three Stages in Action

Example 1: Python (Programming)

Stage 1 – Initial Question (15–20s)

"How did you decide where to place that for loop in your code?"

Stage 2 – Rephrase / Invert (10s)

"What do you think would happen if the loop were outside that block instead?"

Stage 3 – Narrow / Hint (5s)

"Look at which lines are supposed to repeat — which part of the code controls that repetition?"

The peer coach is not explaining syntax — they are guiding the student to examine their own logic and structure.

Why These Skills Matter

✓

Keep ownership of thinking with the student

When you don't jump in, students own their thinking process.

✓

Prevent over-explaining and "teaching mode"

You stay in coaching, where students do the cognitive work.

✓

Build students' confidence and independence

Success through their own thinking builds real confidence.

✓

Encourage deeper processing instead of quick answers

Deeper thinking creates stronger learning.

✓

Support metacognition

Students become aware of how they are thinking and learning.

✓

Normalize that thinking takes time

Multi-stage questioning shows that complex thinking is expected.

Managing the Discomfort of Silence

Silence can feel long to a peer coach. It can feel like you are "doing nothing." In reality, you are doing something very important: holding space for cognition.

Helpful Techniques

  • ✓

    Lightly tap your fingers and count slowly

    (one Mississippi, two Mississippi...)

  • ✓

    Gently tap your foot to pace the seconds

  • ✓

    Slow your breathing and keep an open posture

    Your calm signals to the student that silence is okay.

  • ✓

    Remind yourself: Processing is happening, even if it's quiet

In Summary

1

You ask and wait.

2

You rephrase or invert and wait.

3

You narrow or hint and wait.

As a peer coach, your objective is to not rush, rescue, or resolve.

By tolerating silence and scaffolding gradually, you allow students to do the real work of learning — thinking, connecting, and making meaning for themselves.
