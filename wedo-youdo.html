Week Two Training

We Do, You Do

Practicing and Transferring the Strategy

After the peer coach has modeled a strategy in the "I Do" stage, the focus shifts to helping the student try the process themselves and, eventually, use it independently. This happens through the next two stages: We Do and You Do.

We Do: Guided Practice with Support

The "We Do" stage is where the student begins applying the strategy, with the peer coach staying actively involved as a guide rather than as the person doing the work.

In this stage, the peer coach and student work through a small piece of material together. The student is doing the thinking and the writing, but the peer coach supports the process by:

  • • Using powerful, process-focused questions to help the student reflect on their approach
  • • Allowing wait time so the student has space to think and respond
  • • Using staged questioning to scaffold without taking over
  • • Offering feedback on the student's use of the strategy (not on the correctness of content)
  • • Checking for understanding and clarity along the way

The peer coach does not evaluate the student's academic answers. Instead, feedback is focused on the process, such as:

  • • How they selected key information
  • • How they organized or reworded it
  • • How they used the template or structure of the strategy
  • • How clearly their notes or plan would support later study

The goal is to help the student notice what they are doing, adjust as needed, and gain confidence using the strategy while support is immediately available.

"We do" should be thought of as a spectrum, where more and more of the responsibility is turned over to the student as the coaching session progresses, preparing them for independent practice.

You Do: Independent Application

The "You Do" stage often represents what happens after the session. At this point, the student applies the strategy on their own, without the peer coach present. They use the same thinking processes and tools they witnessed and practiced during "I Do" and "We Do" to:

  • • Engage with new sections of the course
  • • Create their own notes, tables, flashcards, or outlines
  • • Plan their studying
  • • Monitor their understanding
  • • Prepare for assessments

In "You Do," the responsibility fully shifts to the student. The role of the peer coach is to help them leave the session with:

  • ✓ A clear understanding of the strategy
  • ✓ Confidence in how to use it
  • ✓ A plan for where and when they will apply it next

How the Stages Work Together

I Do: The peer coach models the thinking and process.
We Do: The student practices with guidance, prompting, and feedback on strategy use.
You Do: The student carries the strategy forward independently.

Together, these stages support students in moving from observing a learning process, to practicing it with support, to using it on their own in their courses.

Interactive: Identifying Coach Strategies

Below are scenarios showing different coaching approaches. Decide whether each response represents effective "We Do" guidance (supporting the student's thinking process) or ineffective coaching (taking over or focusing on content rather than strategy).

Scenario 1: Recognizing Key Information

Student is reading a dense paragraph and looks confused.

Peer coach says: "I can see you're working through this. What do you notice about how many ideas are packed into this paragraph? And which one feels like the main point to you?"

Scenario 2: Content vs. Strategy Feedback

Student has condensed a definition into their own words.

Peer coach says: "That's not quite right. The actual definition says X, not Y. You need to include all the key terms from the textbook."

Scenario 3: Taking Over vs. Supporting

Student is struggling to organize information into a table format.

Peer coach says: "Here, let me show you. I'll do the first row so you can see how it's done." (Coach takes the pen and fills in the table.)

Scenario 4: Process-Focused Feedback

Student has created flashcards but put too much information on each card.

Peer coach says: "I see you've written a lot on each card. Think about what you'll actually be able to recall quickly when you study. How might changing the amount of information on each card affect how well this works as a study tool?"
