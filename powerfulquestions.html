Powerful Questions in Peer Coaching

Questions that invite reflection and deeper thinking

What Is a Powerful Question?

In coaching, a powerful question is one that helps the student think more deeply about their own experience, approach, and choices. Rather than seeking a specific "right" answer, a powerful question invites reflection, insight, and self-discovery.

Powerful questions are not used to test a student's knowledge, explain content, or lead the student to a conclusion. Their purpose is to help the student slow down, examine how they are thinking or working, and become more aware of what is helping or getting in their way.

A powerful question:

✓

Opens up thinking instead of narrowing it

✓

Encourages the student to reflect rather than defend

✓

Helps the student notice patterns, assumptions, or current/needed strategies

✓

Supports the student in generating their own insights and next steps

What Powerful Questions Focus On

When peer coaches ask powerful questions, they're focusing on process, not content. They think to themselves:

"How can I help this student approach this differently?"

"How can I help this student locate the information they're missing?"

Questions are aimed at how the student is engaging with learning, rather than what the correct answer is.

Powerful questions help the student explore:

→

How the student is interpreting what they read

→

How they decide what is important

→

How they organize and review information

→

How they approach studying and practice

→

How they prepare for and respond to assessments

→

How emotions like stress or confidence affect their performance

What Makes a Question Powerful

Opens Up Thinking

Instead of narrowing options or asking for a single fact, powerful questions invite the student to explore, wonder, and consider multiple perspectives.

Encourages Reflection

Rather than putting students on the defensive, powerful questions invite them to pause, think, and examine their own understanding and approach.

Reveals Patterns

Help students notice what they're doing—their assumptions, habits, and strategies—so they can see what's working and what might need to change.

Builds Independence

Support students in generating their own insights and next steps, moving them from dependence on the coach to self-directed learning.

Teaching Questions vs. Coaching Questions

Teaching / Content-Focused Coaching / Process-Focused
"What are the stages of design thinking?" "How did you try to make sense of the stages when you first read them?"
"What does the CIA triad stand for?" "What part of that section felt hardest to keep straight, and why do you think that is?"
"What's the difference between an IEP and a 504?" "What cues in the text are you using to tell those two apart?"
"Which law covers this situation?" "How are you deciding which details in the reading are most important to remember?"
"What is the definition of integrity in cybersecurity?" "What strategy are you using to turn these definitions into something you can recall on the exam?"

The Pattern: The left column focuses on what the answer is. The right column focuses on how the student is thinking, reading, organizing, and remembering.

How to Formulate a Powerful Question

1

Keep It Open-Ended

Avoid questions that can be answered with "yes," "no," or a single fact.

Less powerful: "Did you understand the chapter?"

More powerful: "What parts of the chapter felt clear, and which parts felt harder to make sense of?"

2

Aim at the Student's Approach, Not the Material

Shift from "what is it?" to "how are you working with it?"

Content-focused: "What are the stages of design thinking?"

Process-focused: "How did you try to keep the stages straight when you studied them?"

3

Invite Reflection, Not Justification

The goal is understanding, not proving.

Less powerful: "Why didn't you study more?"

More powerful: "What was your study plan this week, and how did it end up working for you?"

4

Be Neutral and Non-Leading

Avoid embedding advice or a "right" answer in the question.

Leading: "Have you tried using flashcards?"

More powerful: "What strategies have you experimented with so far to remember this information?"

5

Focus on Awareness and Choice

Powerful questions help students notice what they are doing and consider what they might do differently.

"What did you notice about your focus during your last study session?"

"What seems to help you remember information most effectively?"

"What happens in your thinking when you get stuck on a question?"

Why Powerful Questions Matter

When used well, powerful questions help move students from passively receiving information to actively reflecting on how they learn, which is central to effective peer coaching.

This does not mean closed (yes/no) questions cannot be used, but powerful questions should make up the majority of your questioning.
