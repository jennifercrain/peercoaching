Essential Coaching Skill

Active Listening in Peer Coaching

Master the skill of listening, reflecting, and validating

What Is Active Listening?

Active listening means resisting the urge to immediately explain, fix, reassure, or redirect. Instead, you focus on accurately reflecting what the student is saying, validating their experience, and confirming understanding before the conversation moves forward.

In many sessions, students are not only looking for information on one challenge — they are trying to make sense of confusion, frustration, or anxiety for multiple struggles. How you listen often matters more than what you say next.

Why Active Listening Matters

Without Active Listening

When peer coaches immediately respond with explanations or reassurance:

•

The coach may solve the wrong problem because the real issue hasn't been defined

•

The student stays passive instead of learning to examine their own thinking

•

The real underlying issue remains unaddressed

With Active Listening

By listening first, you help students:

•

Feel understood rather than judged

•

Slow down and organize their thoughts

•

Clarify what the real issues actually are

•

Build trust in the coaching partnership

Core Elements of Active Listening

Active listening typically involves four key moves that help students clarify and organize their thinking.

1. Reflecting Content

Restating, in your own words, what the student has shared. This shows you're paying attention and helps them hear their own thinking reflected back.

2. Validating Experience

Acknowledging that the student's confusion, frustration, or anxiety makes sense in context. Validation builds trust and shows empathy.

3. Checking for Accuracy

Giving the student a chance to confirm, clarify, or correct your understanding. This ensures you've truly understood before moving forward.

4. Inviting Elaboration

Gently encouraging the student to add more detail in a supportive, non-interrogative way. Open-ended questions help them go deeper.

How Students Communicate Their Struggles

Students often speak in general, emotional, or incomplete statements. Active listening helps you understand what's really going on beneath the surface.

❝ I just don't get linear equations. ❞

What this might mean: Content confusion, frustration, or math anxiety

❝ Accounting doesn't make sense at all. ❞

What this might mean: Feeling overwhelmed by a subject area, not just one topic

❝ I always mix up IEPs and 504s. ❞

What this might mean: Difficulty distinguishing between similar concepts

❝ I get so nervous on OAs that my mind goes blank. ❞

What this might mean: Test anxiety, not necessarily content mastery issues

Why This Step Comes First

Validation and reflection help students feel understood, slow down and organize their thoughts, clarify what the real issues are, and build trust in the coaching partnership.

Only after the student confirms that your understanding is accurate does it make sense to move into strategy, redirection, or skill-building. Active listening creates the foundation that all other coaching skills build on.
